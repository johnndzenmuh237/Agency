Why Most Rebrands Fail in the First 90 Days

Article Cover Image

Every failed rebrand we've been called in to fix looked great on launch day. The deck was tight, the new logo photographed well, and the internal team was proud of what shipped. Ninety days later, half of them had quietly reverted to old templates, old taglines, or both.

The pattern is consistent enough that we now treat it as the real risk in any identity project — not the design itself, but the gap between launch and adoption.

The guidelines document isn't the system

A PDF of logo specs tells your team what not to do. It rarely tells them what to do next time they need a new social template, a conference banner, or a hiring page. Without that second layer — templates, examples, and a person who owns the brand internally — teams default back to what's familiar within a quarter.

A brand system survives contact with a deadline, or it doesn't survive at all.

Three things that predict adoption

Across the rebrands that stuck, three conditions showed up almost every time. First, someone internal was named as the brand owner before launch, not after. Second, the most-used templates — decks, social posts, one-pagers — existed in finished form on day one, not as an afterthought. Third, leadership used the new identity publicly within the first two weeks, which gave everyone else permission to do the same.

What we changed in our own process

We now build a 90-day adoption plan into every identity engagement, not just a delivery date. That includes a short internal training session, a template library scoped to what the client's team actually produces weekly, and a 60-day check-in to catch drift before it becomes a pattern.

None of this is complicated. It's just usually the first thing cut when a project runs over budget — and it's the part that determines whether the work outlives the launch party.
